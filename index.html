Senso-ji Temple · 5 min walk · go early before crowds
Tokyo's oldest temple, founded in 645 AD. The iconic Kaminarimon (Thunder Gate) with its massive red lantern leads to Nakamise Street — a covered arcade of souvenir stalls. The main hall enshrines a golden image of Kannon, the goddess of mercy. Go before 8 AM — serene and misty before the crowds arrive.

Sumo is Japan's national sport with roots going back over 1,500 years. The May Tournament (Natsu Basho) is one of six annual grand tournaments. Chanko nabe is the protein-rich hot pot stew that sumo wrestlers eat daily — rich broth loaded with chicken, tofu, vegetables, and noodles. Many former wrestlers run chanko restaurants in Ryogoku. No beef — naturally chicken and tofu-based. No reservation needed. Perfect post-sumo dinner.

Fushimi Inari · thousands of red torii gates · go early · allow 2+ hrs
One of Japan's most iconic sights — thousands of vermillion torii gates snaking up a sacred mountain. Dedicated to Inari, the Shinto god of rice, the shrine dates to 711 AD. The tunnel of gates stretches 4km to the summit. Even 45 minutes up gives you the magic. Go early — by 9am tour groups arrive in force.

Gion district · traditional townhouses · lanterns at dusk
Kyoto's most famous geisha district — preserved wooden machiya townhouses lining cobblestone lanes. Geiko and maiko can be spotted heading to evening engagements in full kimono. The area flourished in the 17th century during Kyoto's cultural golden age.
Japan Guide — Gion📍 Google Maps

Arashiyama Bamboo Grove · go early before crowds
A path through towering moso bamboo — the stalks grow so densely they block the sky, creating an otherworldly green tunnel. Early morning is magical: silent except for the wind rustling the bamboo. One of Japan's most photographed landscapes.

Less crowded than the bamboo grove. Your serene, contemplative, movie-moment temple.

8:45 AM
Nara Deer Park + Todai-ji · walk 10–15 min from station
Around 1,200 freely roaming Sika deer considered sacred messengers of the gods since the 8th century. They bow in exchange for shika senbei (deer crackers, ¥150/pack). One of Japan's most unique and joyful experiences. Keep food hidden when not feeding — deer can be assertive!
Japan Guide — Nara Park📍 Google Maps
Built in 745 AD, Todai-ji houses the world's largest bronze Buddha — nearly 15 meters tall. The Great Buddha Hall (Daibutsuden) is the world's largest wooden building. Famous pillar with a hole the size of the Buddha's nostril — kids who squeeze through are granted enlightenment (it's tight — the boys can try!). Allow 60–90 minutes.

Dotonbori · late lunch · takoyaki, kushikatsu, ramen
Osaka's legendary neon entertainment and food district. Birthplace of takoyaki (octopus balls) — try them hot from a street stall. Also try kushikatsu (deep-fried skewers — don't double-dip!). The Glico Running Man billboard is the icon. Osaka's food culture (kuidaore — eat til you drop) is the best in Japan.
Japan Guide — Dotonbori📍 Google Maps

Kuromon Ichiba Market · 10 min walk from Dotonbori
Osaka's famous fresh food market — nicknamed "Osaka's Kitchen." Hundreds of stalls selling fresh seafood, Wagyu skewers (for Rohan!), produce, and street snacks. More authentic and local than Dotonbori. Allow 30–45 minutes of browsing and snacking.
Japan Guide — Kuromon Market📍 Google Maps
3:30 PM
Osaka Castle · 20 min by subway
One of Japan's most famous castles — a stunning five-story tower surrounded by enormous stone walls and moats. Originally built by Toyotomi Hideyoshi in 1583. The surrounding park is beautiful in late May — lush green grounds. The interior museum tells the story of Osaka's unification of Japan. Allow 90 minutes.
Japan Guide — Osaka Castle📍 Google Maps
5:30 PM
Shinsekai · retro 1920s neighbourhood · 15 min from castle
A fascinatingly retro neighbourhood built in the 1920s — modelled on Coney Island and Paris. Home to Tsutenkaku Tower and dozens of kushikatsu restaurants. Completely different vibe from Dotonbori — gritty, atmospheric, authentically local Osaka.
Japan Guide — Shinsekai📍 Google Maps
6:30 PM
Kushikatsu dinner in Shinsekai · walk-in · no reservation needed
Kushikatsu was born in Shinsekai — skewered meat, vegetables, and seafood breaded and deep-fried, served with a communal dipping sauce. The golden rule: NEVER double-dip. Dip once, eat, move on. Signs everywhere remind you. Casual, fun, very Osaka. Walk in anywhere that looks busy.

Three beautifully preserved streets of Edo-period merchant houses — dark-wooded facades, sake breweries, craft shops selling lacquerware, ceramics, and Hida folk crafts. Takayama prospered as a centre of skilled carpentry under direct Tokugawa shogunate rule. Feels completely authentic, not touristy. Best in morning light or at golden hour.

Shirakawa-go · UNESCO thatched-roof villages · ~1hr by bus
A remote mountain village of gassho-zukuri farmhouses — massive steep thatched roofs designed to shed heavy snow, stacked four or five floors high. UNESCO World Heritage since 1995. Late May brings lush green rice paddies surrounding the village. One of Japan's most scenic and culturally rich landscapes.

Japan's steepest mountain railway using a switchback system. In early June, thousands of hydrangeas bloom in blue and purple along the tracks. The journey from Odawara to Gora takes about 55 minutes. Fully covered by Hakone Free Pass.
Japan Guide — Hakone📍 Google Maps

Arrive Gora · taxi to Hotel Indigo (~10 min · ¥1,500) · check in · onsen
Hakone sits on a volcanic caldera, producing naturally geothermally heated mineral-rich water. Soaking in onsen is a deeply Japanese ritual — different minerals treat different ailments. Your hotel has onsen access. Etiquette: wash thoroughly before entering, no swimwear, tattoos may be an issue at public facilities (hotel onsen is usually fine).

Morning
Owakudani Ropeway · volcanic valley cable car
A cable car that floats above an active volcanic valley — sulfur vents billow white smoke from below. Buy kuro-tamago (black eggs hard-boiled in sulfurous springs — each adds 7 years to your life!). Covered by Hakone Free Pass.
Japan Guide — Owakudani📍 Google Maps
Mt Fuji is visible from Owakudani on clear days — June visibility is roughly 20% due to early rainy season. Don't make it the goal — treat it as a bonus. Even without Fuji, Owakudani's volcanic landscape is extraordinary.

How Takkyubin Works
